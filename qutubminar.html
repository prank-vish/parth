About Qutub Minar

The Qutub Minar, located in Delhi, India, is the tallest brick minaret in the world. It was built by Qutb-ud-din Aibak in 1193 to celebrate Muslim dominance in Delhi after the defeat of Delhi's last Hindu ruler.

The Qutub Minar is 73 meters tall and consists of five distinct storeys, each marked by a projecting balcony. It is adorned with intricate carvings and verses from the Quran.

This UNESCO World Heritage site attracts numerous visitors who admire its architectural beauty and historical significance.

Architecture

The Qutub Minar's architecture is a blend of Islamic and Persian styles. Its red sandstone and marble structure is decorated with intricate carvings and inscriptions in Arabic. The minaret tapers from 2.75 meters in diameter at its peak to 14.32 meters at its base, showcasing the skilled craftsmanship of its builders.

History

The Qutub Minar was commissioned by Qutb-ud-din Aibak, the first ruler of the Delhi Sultanate, and later completed by his successor Iltutmish. It stands as a symbol of the beginning of Muslim rule in India and has witnessed several restorations over the centuries due to lightning strikes and earthquakes.
